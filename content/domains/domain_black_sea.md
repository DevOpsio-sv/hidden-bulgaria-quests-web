## Concept

The Black Sea coast of Bulgaria is one of the oldest continuously inhabited shorelines in Europe. Greek colonies, Thracian tribes, Roman ports, Byzantine monasteries, and Ottoman fortifications all left their mark on this narrow strip between sea and steppe. This domain tells the story of the coast as a border — between empires, cultures, civilizations — and the memory that survived on the water's edge.

---

## Historical Epoch

**Before the Name (Преди Името)** — pre-681 AD, though routes extend into the medieval period.

The coast was inhabited and significant long before the Bulgarian state was founded. Greek colonies established Apollonia (Sozopol), Messembria (Nessebar), and Odessos (Varna) in the 6th century BC. The routes explore this layered history.

## The Three Routes

### Route 1 — `route_black_sea_northern`
**Working title:** The Northern Gate  
**Narrative:** The ancient Greek colony at Odessos (Varna), the Varna Gold (the world's oldest processed gold — 6500 years old), and the transition from Thracian to Greek to Roman to Byzantine civilization on the northern coast.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Varna Archaeological Museum (Varna Gold context)
- Varna Roman Baths (largest in Bulgaria)
- Aladzha Monastery (cave monastery, 12th century)
- Cape Kaliakra (fortress on the sea cliff)
- Balchik (botanical garden and palace — Ottoman/European layer)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_northern_gate_seal`

---

### Route 2 — `route_black_sea_central`
**Working title:** The Island Memory  
**Narrative:** Nessebar (ancient Messembria) — a UNESCO World Heritage Site that has been continuously inhabited for over 3200 years. Sozopol (ancient Apollonia) — the oldest Greek colony on the western Black Sea, founded 610 BC.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Nessebar old town gate (the narrow isthmus entrance)
- Church of Christ Pantocrator, Nessebar
- Sozopol old town harbor wall
- St. Kirik and St. Julitta monastery island, Sozopol
- Sozopol archaeological museum (the relics of St. John the Baptist — the Sozopol relics controversy)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_ancient_harbor_token`

---

### Route 3 — `route_black_sea_southern`
**Working title:** The Salt and Stone  
**Narrative:** The southern coast between Burgas and the Turkish border — salt lakes, wetlands, ancient roads, and the transition point where the Black Sea coast becomes the border of Europe.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Burgas Lakes (one of the most important bird migration sites in Europe)
- Ancient Ponetica (Pomorie) — oldest city still bearing its ancient name on Bulgaria's coast
- Ahtopol — the southernmost Bulgarian coastal town, once a Byzantine fortress
- Rezovo River mouth — the southernmost point of Bulgaria
- Strandzha Nature Park — ancient Thracian ritual sites

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_sea_memory`

## Domain Seal

**Seal ID:** `seal_black_sea`  
**Working concept:** A circular seal combining a trireme prow, a salt crystal formation, and the Thracian spiral motif found on Black Sea coastal artifacts — representing the three civilizations (Greek, Thracian, Bulgarian) that left permanent marks on this coast.

---

## Narrative Arc Across the Domain

**Act 1 (Northern route):** The oldest gold in the world found here. The sea as a cradle.  
**Act 2 (Central route):** The island town that never stopped being a port. Continuous civilization as survival.  
**Act 3 (Southern route):** The coast as the edge of the known world — and then as the edge of Bulgaria.

The domain's answer to the core question: *The Black Sea coast survived because it was too valuable to be ignored. Every empire used it, and in using it, was forced to deal with the people who lived there — and those people carried their stories forward.*
