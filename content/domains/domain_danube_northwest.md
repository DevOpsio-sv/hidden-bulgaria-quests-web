## Concept

The northwestern corner of Bulgaria is where the Roman Empire met the Danube — and where that empire's edge became, centuries later, the northern border of the Bulgarian state. This domain explores the deep pre-Bulgarian history of the land: Roman fortresses, the extraordinary Belogradchik rock formations, ancient roads, and the river that has always been both barrier and bridge. It answers the question of what existed here before the name — and why the name, when it arrived, stayed.

---

## Historical Epoch

**Before the Name (Преди Името)** — with content extending into the early Bulgarian period.

The Danube frontier defined the northern limit of the Roman Empire for centuries. The Via Diagonalis — the great Roman road — passed through this region. Many of Bulgaria's oldest continuously inhabited towns began as Roman fortifications along this route.

## The Three Routes

### Route 1 — `route_danube_northwest_belogradchik`
**Working title:** The Stone Guardians  
**Narrative:** The Belogradchik rock formations — natural sandstone pillars up to 200 metres high — were used as a fortress by Romans, Byzantines, and Bulgarians in succession. The rocks are older than any civilization that named them, but each civilization found them impossible to ignore.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Belogradchik Fortress (built into the rocks)
- Belogradchik rock formation viewpoint (the "Horseman" and "Madonna")
- Ledenika Cave (active cave system, archaeological finds)
- Vratsa Museum (Thracian gold — the Rogozen treasure, largest Thracian silver hoard found in Bulgaria)
- Vratsa Gorge (natural canyon, Roman road traces)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_stone_guardian_seal`

---

### Route 2 — `route_danube_northwest_roman_roads`
**Working title:** The Road That Never Slept  
**Narrative:** The Via Diagonalis connected Constantinople to the Danube. It passed through what is now northwestern Bulgaria as the empire's primary overland route. The Roman towns along this road — Oescus, Ulpia Oescus, Montana — show how deeply the land was integrated into imperial civilization before Bulgaria existed.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Gigen (site of ancient Oescus — major Roman city)
- Montana (Roman fortress town Montana)
- Vratsa Roman road section (visible paving stones)
- Mezdra area (Roman crossing point)
- Danube observation point at Kozloduy (where the river defines the border)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_roman_road_mile_stone`

---

### Route 3 — `route_danube_northwest_vidin`
**Working title:** The Last Fortress  
**Narrative:** Vidin — the ancient Bononia — has been continuously fortified for over 2000 years. The Baba Vida fortress, the only fully preserved medieval castle in Bulgaria, sits on the Danube bank and tells the story of the northwestern frontier from Roman through medieval Bulgarian to Ottoman periods.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Baba Vida Fortress, Vidin (the castle itself)
- Vidin Synagogue (one of the best-preserved synagogues in the Balkans — a layer of the multicultural Danube past)
- Vidin Danube riverbank (the view into Romania — where the river was both border and trade route)
- Vidin Archaeological Museum
- Magura Cave (one of Bulgaria's most important prehistoric art sites — cave paintings 8000–4000 BC)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_danube_frontier_key`

## Domain Seal

**Seal ID:** `seal_danube_northwest`  
**Working concept:** The seal combines the silhouette of the Belogradchik rocks, a Roman eagle motif from the Rogozen treasure, and a simplified Danube wave pattern — representing natural formation, Roman layer, and the river boundary all in one.

## Narrative Arc Across the Domain

**Act 1 (Belogradchik route):** The land before any civilization. Stone that predates names.  
**Act 2 (Roman roads route):** The road that knit this land into an empire — and the towns that grew along it.  
**Act 3 (Vidin route):** The frontier fortress that survived every empire that used it, and still stands.

The domain's answer to the core question: *This land was ancient before Bulgaria was born. The Roman roads, the Danube river, the rock formations — they were here first. Bulgaria absorbed all of this into itself, turning pre-Bulgarian heritage into Bulgarian memory. The name survived because it was flexible enough to contain what came before it.*
