## Concept

Sofia is the youngest major European capital but sits on one of the oldest crossroads on the continent. Serdica — the Roman city beneath Sofia — was Constantine the Great's favourite city ("Serdica is my Rome"). Below Serdica are Thracian settlements. Below that, Neolithic habitation. The city has never stopped being lived in, never stopped accumulating memory. Sofia also serves as the gateway to western Bulgaria — the Vitosha mountain, the medieval Rila and Boyana monasteries, and the mountain passes through which every conquering army had to pass to reach or leave the Balkan peninsula.

---

## Historical Epoch

**After the Name (След Името)** — from the founding of the Bulgarian state through the medieval and National Revival periods to the modern capital.

Sofia became the Bulgarian capital only in 1879, after centuries as a relatively minor regional town under Ottoman rule. Yet the city's layers — Roman, Byzantine, Bulgarian medieval, Ottoman, modern — make it the most compressed example of the survival story in the entire country.

## The Three Routes

### Route 1 — `route_sofia_west_underground`
**Working title:** The City Beneath the City  
**Narrative:** Walking through central Sofia, you are walking over one of the most archaeologically significant urban landscapes in Europe. The Serdica metro station and archaeological complex reveals the Roman city at street level. The rotunda of Saint George — the oldest functioning church in Sofia, built by the Romans in the 4th century — sits in the courtyard of a modern hotel. Every layer is accessible.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Serdica Archaeological Complex (Roman city, accessible in the Sofia metro)
- Rotunda of Saint George (4th century Roman rotunda — oldest building in Sofia still in active use)
- National Archaeological Museum Sofia (former Ottoman mosque building — layers within layers)
- Saint Nedelya Square (where Bulgarian kings were crowned in medieval times — now a city square)
- Largo area (Soviet-era architecture over Roman remains — the political layer)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_serdica_foundation_stone`

---

### Route 2 — `route_sofia_west_vitosha`
**Working title:** The Mountain That Watches the City  
**Narrative:** Vitosha mountain — the city's natural landmark and ancient park — has watched Sofia through every historical period. Medieval monasteries on its slopes housed Bulgarian manuscripts when the state was under occupation. The mountain's springs fed the Roman city. Its paths were used by Ottoman merchants, Bulgarian revolutionaries, and Soviet-era hikers. Vitosha is a living archive of every age of Sofia's history.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Vitosha mountain — Kopitoto peak viewpoint (panoramic view of Sofia against the mountain backdrop)
- Boyana Church (UNESCO World Heritage — 13th century Bulgarian frescoes, among the finest medieval paintings in the world)
- Dragalevtsi Monastery (mountain monastery where Bulgarian manuscripts were hidden during Ottoman rule)
- Stone River natural monument (unique geological formation)
- Vitosha Natural Park visitor centre (ecology and archaeology of the mountain)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_manuscript_page_fragment`

---

### Route 3 — `route_sofia_west_rila`
**Working title:** The House of Memory  
**Narrative:** Rila Monastery — the largest and most important Bulgarian Orthodox monastery, a UNESCO World Heritage Site — is the single most important cultural institution in Bulgarian history. During the Ottoman period, it preserved manuscripts, education, and Bulgarian language when no Bulgarian state existed. It is not a relic of the past — it is an active monastery that has been continuously inhabited since the 10th century.  
**Key checkpoint locations (draft):**
- Rila Monastery main courtyard (UNESCO — the painted facade is one of the most recognizable images in Bulgarian culture)
- Rila Monastery museum (the Hrelyu Tower — 14th century, the oldest surviving structure)
- Ivan Rilski hermitage (cave where the monastery's founder lived — a short hike from the monastery)
- Rila Mountain pass (the physical journey through the mountain as part of the story)
- Blagoevgrad (regional city, Bulgarian National Revival architecture, Bachinovo park area)

**Route Relic (draft):** `relic_rila_manuscript_seal`

## Domain Seal

**Seal ID:** `seal_sofia_west`  
**Working concept:** The seal combines the Rotunda of Saint George's circular plan (the Roman layer), the painted Rila Monastery facade pattern (the Bulgarian preservation layer), and the Vitosha mountain silhouette (the natural witness layer) — three circles nested together, representing the three timescales compressed in this domain.

## Narrative Arc Across the Domain

**Act 1 (Underground route):** What was here before Bulgaria. The Roman city that became the foundation.  
**Act 2 (Vitosha route):** What the mountain watched. The preservation of memory in the mountain's monasteries.  
**Act 3 (Rila route):** The house where the name was kept alive when there was no state to keep it.

The domain's answer to the core question: *The name survived because institutions like Rila Monastery refused to let the language and memory die. When there was no Bulgarian state, the monastery was the state of Bulgarian culture. The name survived in manuscripts, in education, in the art on these walls — and the walls are still standing.*

## Content Production Notes

- Boyana Church (UNESCO) frescoes from 1259 are considered among the finest examples of medieval European painting — they predate the Italian Renaissance and show a naturalistic style that was extraordinary for their time. International audiences unfamiliar with Bulgarian art will find this revelatory.
- Rila Monastery is the single most visited cultural site in Bulgaria (alongside Plovdiv's old town) — highest recognition factor for marketing materials
- The Serdica/Sofia layers story ("Roma is my Rome" — Constantine's quote) has strong hook for visitors who have been to Rome and can connect the story
- The "underground Sofia" route has high visual impact for AR potential — pointing a camera at a modern street and seeing the Roman layer beneath is one of the strongest Guardian Sight sequences conceivable
- Rila Monastery UNESCO status (since 1983) is well-established internationally
